## How to Read This

This wiki is organized into three sections, and the sections are not interchangeable. We want to be explicit about the distinction up front so that you can read each article with the right expectations.

**Section 1 is descriptive.** The articles explain how parts of the political, legal, and economic system actually work — state legislative process, lobbying, collective action, institutions, deliberative democracy, democratic backsliding, nonprofit law, market failure. These articles are meant to be as neutral as a serious treatment of the subject allows. They do not recommend policies. They do not advocate for the platform. Where the underlying empirical or theoretical literature is contested, we say so using inline flags (**[Contested]**, **[Evidence mixed]**, **[We don't know]**). Where a specific statistic or claim needs verification against a primary source, it is flagged with `[VERIFY]` and recorded in the Fact-Check Checklist at the end of the wiki. If you find Section 1 articles reading like advocacy for the platform, that is a mistake we want to hear about.

**Section 2 is the founders' perspective.** The articles in this section explain why we are building this platform, what we think the problem is, what we think our design is doing about it, and what failure modes we are trying to avoid. They are written in first person plural ("we") and they are labeled as the founders' view on every article. They are not neutral and they do not pretend to be. They are meant to be honest about what we believe, what we are uncertain about, and where our reasoning could turn out to be wrong. You do not need to agree with any of it to use the platform. You should know what the people who built it think.

**Section 3 is about what the platform deliberately does not answer.** It explains the distinction between empirical questions (where evidence can in principle resolve the dispute) and values questions (where reasonable people disagree based on different weightings of legitimate goods), and it commits the platform to different treatment of each. This section is short because its main job is to hold us to a standard, not to generate content. If you find the platform pre-loading answers to questions that belong in this section, the right response is to call it out.

**A note on length and effort.** The articles are long because the subjects are genuinely complicated and the history of civic technology is littered with projects that failed in part because they oversimplified. That said, you do not need to read this wiki to participate. If you want the short version, Article 2.1 is the core diagnosis and Article 2.2 is the core response. Everything else is elaboration.

**A note on corrections.** We expect to revise these articles as we learn more, as the platform accumulates operating experience, and as participants point out where we have gotten something wrong. The wiki is a working document, not a finished one. Every article has a sources section at the bottom, and the Fact-Check Checklist at the end lists specific empirical claims that need primary-source verification before we treat any of this as settled.

## Article 1.1: How a Bill Becomes Law (State Level)

**Section 1: How Things Work**

Most Americans learn the federal version of this story in school: a bill is introduced, debated, voted on by both chambers, reconciled, signed by the president. The state version rhymes with that story but differs in ways that matter for anyone trying to influence outcomes. And state processes vary significantly from one another — what follows is a general model, with the most common variations flagged. **[Evidence mixed]** on any specific state's procedural details; verify locally before acting on them.

A bill in a state legislature begins with a sponsor — a single legislator, or sometimes a group, who introduces text drafted by their staff, by a lobbyist, by a constituent, by a model-legislation organization, or by the governor's office. The drafting origin is rarely visible on the face of the bill itself, and this matters: a bill's text may already reflect the priorities of an organized interest before any public deliberation begins.

Once introduced, the bill is referred to a committee. This is the first place most bills die. Across most state legislatures, the majority of introduced bills never receive a committee hearing, let alone a vote. The committee chair — appointed by legislative leadership — has substantial discretion over whether to schedule a hearing. A bill that the chair declines to hear is, for practical purposes, dead, regardless of its merits or its sponsor's enthusiasm. This procedural fact is the single most important thing to understand about state legislative process: the agenda is not set by the full body, it is set by leadership and committee chairs. Alan Rosenthal documented the long arc of this trend in *The Decline of Representative Democracy* (1998), arguing that legislative leadership has accumulated agenda-setting power at the expense of rank-and-file members and committee independence over recent decades.

If a bill clears committee, it moves to the floor of its chamber of origin. Floor debate is structured by chamber rules that vary widely: some states allow extensive amendment from the floor, others tightly restrict it. A successful floor vote sends the bill to the second chamber, where the entire process — committee referral, hearings, floor vote — repeats. If the second chamber amends the bill, the two versions must be reconciled. Many states use a conference committee, a small group of members from each chamber who negotiate a single text, which is then sent back to both chambers for an up-or-down vote with no further amendment. Conference committees often meet in private, and the final text can differ substantially from anything either chamber originally passed.

The governor's role varies. Every state governor can sign or veto a bill that reaches their desk. Most states allow a legislative override of a veto, though the supermajority required varies. Some governors have additional powers: a **line-item veto**, allowing the governor to strike specific provisions of an appropriations bill while signing the rest, exists in most states for budget bills. An **amendatory veto**, which allows the governor to send a bill back with proposed changes, exists in a smaller number of states (Illinois is the most well-known example). These powers significantly affect bargaining dynamics during the legislative process.

A separate track — and one that is easy to overlook — is **regulatory rulemaking**. Many policies that look like legislation are actually written by executive-branch agencies under authority delegated by a statute. The legislature passes a general law; an agency writes the detailed rules that determine what the law means in practice. Rulemaking processes typically require public notice, a comment period, and a final rule, all of which create points of intervention. The pharmaceutical, environmental, financial, and labor domains all involve substantial rulemaking activity that occurs without any further legislative vote. For anyone trying to influence policy, ignoring the rulemaking track means ignoring much of where policy is actually made.

**State legislatures differ from Congress in several ways that affect strategy.** Most state legislators are part-time and have small staffs — sometimes none. This means individual legislators are more accessible to constituents and lobbyists alike, and that the information environment around any given bill is shaped by whoever shows up with credible-looking analysis. Sessions are often short (some states meet only a few months a year, a few meet only every other year), which compresses the window for influence. Term limits exist in roughly fifteen states **[VERIFY: NCSL current count]**, which weakens institutional memory and increases the relative power of permanent staff and lobbyists.

### What this means for deliberation

Knowing where bills die is more useful than knowing how they pass. Most policy fights are won or lost in committee, in the rulemaking process, or in the agenda-setting decisions that determine which bills get heard at all. A platform that aggregates citizen positions on a bill that will never receive a hearing is producing an artifact with no destination. Effective civic deliberation has to be timed to the actual legislative calendar and targeted at the actual decision points, which are rarely the floor votes that get the most attention.

## Article 1.2: How Lobbying Works

**Section 1: How Things Work**

The popular image of lobbying — bags of cash, smoke-filled rooms, quid pro quo bribes — is mostly wrong. Modern lobbying is more boring, more legal, and in many ways more effective than the caricature suggests. Understanding what lobbyists actually do is essential for anyone trying to participate in politics on something other than the lobbyists' terms.

A working lobbyist's day is built around relationships and information. They meet with legislators and staff, often briefly and often repeatedly. They provide draft bill text, talking points, research summaries, and lists of supporters and opponents. They attend hearings, testify when invited, and draft testimony for friendly witnesses. They monitor the legislative calendar, track amendments in real time, and tell their clients which votes are scheduled and which way they're likely to go. Much of the value a lobbyist provides is informational: legislators have limited staff and are voting on hundreds of bills, and a lobbyist who can explain a complex piece of legislation in five minutes is genuinely useful to a busy office.

This is what "relationship capital" means. A lobbyist who has worked a state capitol for twenty years knows which staffer drafts the chair's amendments, which legislator has a personal connection to a given issue, which committee members are persuadable and which are locked in. This knowledge cannot be replicated by reading public documents. It is built by being present, being reliable, and not wasting people's time. A first-time advocate walking into the same building has access to the same formal procedures but to almost none of the informal infrastructure that determines how those procedures play out.

Lobbying divides into two broad categories. **Direct lobbying** is communication with legislators or staff intended to influence specific legislation. **Grassroots lobbying** is communication with the public intended to mobilize citizens to contact legislators. Both are legal. Both are constitutionally protected — the right "to petition the Government for a redress of grievances" is in the First Amendment, and the Supreme Court has repeatedly affirmed that paid lobbying falls within it.

Both are also regulated. The federal Lobbying Disclosure Act of 1995 requires lobbyists who meet certain thresholds to register with Congress and file periodic reports disclosing their clients, the issues they lobbied on, and approximate spending. Every state has some analog, though the thresholds, definitions, and reporting requirements vary widely. The disclosure regime is real but porous: many activities that look like lobbying — strategic advice, coalition-building, media campaigns — fall outside the legal definition of "lobbying" and are not reported. When you read that a company "spent $X on lobbying," that figure usually represents only the activities that triggered formal disclosure, not the total spent on political influence.

The numbers themselves are large. According to OpenSecrets, total federal lobbying expenditures reached approximately **$3.7 billion in 2023**, and the pharmaceutical and health products sector alone spent approximately **$374 million in federal lobbying that year** — the largest of any sector. State lobbying expenditures, harder to aggregate because of varied disclosure regimes, add several billion more. **[VERIFY: OpenSecrets 2023 totals, exact figures and sector ranking]**

The "revolving door" describes the movement of personnel between government service and the lobbying industry. Former legislators, congressional staffers, executive branch officials, and regulators frequently take jobs with lobbying firms, trade associations, or the industries they previously regulated. The reverse also occurs. There are cooling-off periods in some jurisdictions, but enforcement is uneven and exceptions are common. The revolving door matters not because of any single transaction but because it shapes career incentives: a regulator who expects to work for a regulated industry has reasons to maintain good relationships with that industry that have nothing to do with corruption in the legal sense.

**The relationship between lobbying spending and legislative outcomes is empirically contested.** This is important. The most thorough study of the question is Frank Baumgartner and colleagues' *Lobbying and Policy Change: Who Wins, Who Loses, and Why* (2009), which tracked nearly a hundred policy issues over four years. Their headline finding was that **the status quo is the most common outcome**: most lobbying is defensive, most issues see no change, and the side with more resources does not always win. They argue that lobbying expenditure is a poor predictor of outcome on any individual issue, though concentrated resources do shift the long-run terms of debate.

A separate and frequently cited paper, Martin Gilens and Benjamin Page's "Testing Theories of American Politics" (2014), argued that policy outcomes correlate strongly with the preferences of economic elites and organized interest groups, and weakly or not at all with the preferences of average citizens. This finding has been widely circulated. **[Contested]** It has also been disputed: Robert Erikson (2015) and Omar Bashir (2015) reanalyzed the same data and argued that the elite-citizen preference correlation is high enough that you cannot statistically distinguish whose preferences are "really" driving outcomes. The debate is technical, ongoing, and the platform takes no position on which side is right — but anyone citing the Gilens & Page result should know it has been challenged on its own terms.

### What this means for deliberation

Lobbying is not the enemy of citizen participation — it is the dominant channel through which information reaches legislators, and it is constitutionally protected. The question is not how to abolish lobbying but how to make a different kind of voice audible alongside it. This platform exists in part because the resource asymmetry between organized economic interests and unorganized citizens is real, even if it is harder to measure than headline lobbying figures suggest, and even if money does not mechanically buy votes.

## Article 1.3: The Logic of Collective Action

**Section 1: How Things Work**

In 1965, an economist named Mancur Olson published *The Logic of Collective Action*, which made a simple argument with disturbing implications. Groups, Olson said, do not automatically organize to pursue their shared interests. Whether they organize depends on size and structure, and the math runs in a counterintuitive direction: small groups with concentrated stakes will organize easily, while large groups with diffuse stakes — even when their total stakes are much higher — will struggle to organize at all.

The reasoning is straightforward. Imagine a public good, like clean air or a more efficient regulation, that would benefit a million people by $100 each — a billion-dollar collective benefit. For any single individual, the cost of organizing, lobbying, contributing time and money, may exceed $100. From that individual's perspective, the rational move is to let someone else do the organizing and enjoy the benefit anyway. This is the **free-rider problem**. Because the good is non-excludable — you get clean air whether you contributed or not — every individual has an incentive to let others bear the cost. If everyone reasons this way, no one organizes, and the good is not produced.

Now imagine a different group: ten companies, each of whom would gain $10 million from a regulatory change worth $100 million collectively. The per-actor stake is enormous. The number of actors is small enough that they can monitor each other. Free-riding is harder when there are only ten of you and everyone notices who did and didn't contribute. This group will organize. They will hire lobbyists, fund research, build coalitions, and pursue the regulatory change with focused energy.

The disturbing implication is that the political system systematically over-represents concentrated interests relative to diffuse ones, even when the diffuse interests are larger in total. This is not because anyone is corrupt. It is because rational individuals respond to their own incentives, and the incentives facing concentrated and diffuse groups are not symmetric. Olson called this the **intensity asymmetry**, and it shows up across nearly every policy domain.

**Pharmaceutical pricing** is a textbook case. A single drug manufacturer has tens or hundreds of millions of dollars riding on a specific pricing or patent provision. Patients paying out of pocket for that drug face costs that are real and painful but distributed across millions of individuals, each of whom faces high costs and low individual political efficacy in trying to change the policy.

**Agricultural subsidies** are another. The American Farm Bureau and commodity-specific trade associations represent a small number of producers with concentrated stakes in the structure of farm support programs. The cost of those programs is paid by consumers and taxpayers in amounts spread across hundreds of millions of grocery bills and tax returns. No individual consumer feels the cost sharply enough to invest in changing it.

**Occupational licensing** illustrates the same dynamic in the labor market. Incumbent practitioners in a licensed occupation — cosmetologists, interior designers, locksmiths — have a concentrated interest in maintaining licensing requirements that limit competition. Prospective entrants and consumers have a diffuse interest in lower barriers and lower prices, but no individual entrant or consumer has enough at stake to organize against the incumbent associations.

**Zoning** works the same way at the local level. Existing homeowners in a neighborhood have a concentrated interest in restrictive zoning that protects property values. Prospective residents — people who do not yet live in the neighborhood and may never know they were excluded — have no organized voice at all. They are not at the planning meeting because they are not yet residents.

Olson's solution to the free-rider problem was the concept of **selective incentives**: private benefits offered exclusively to members of an organization, which give individuals a non-collective reason to join. AARP's insurance discounts are selective incentives. Union health benefits and grievance representation are selective incentives. Member magazines, exclusive events, and professional credentials all serve the same function. The collective political effort is funded indirectly, by people who joined for the private benefits and tolerate the political activity as a side effect.

**Olson's framework has been extended and challenged.** The most important challenge came from Elinor Ostrom, whose *Governing the Commons* (1990) documented dozens of real-world cases in which communities solved collective action problems without state coercion and without the privatization of the resource. Ostrom did not refute Olson — she identified the institutional conditions under which his pessimistic prediction does and does not hold. Her conditions include clear boundaries around the group, locally adapted rules, monitoring by members, graduated sanctions for violators, and accessible conflict resolution. Where these conditions exist, collective action is achievable. Where they do not, Olson's pessimism applies. **[Evidence mixed]** on how readily Ostrom's conditions can be engineered into a new institution rather than emerging organically.

A real-world case study in collective action collapse is the long decline of American labor unions. According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, U.S. union density fell from approximately **35% of wage and salary workers in the mid-1950s** to **10.1% in 2022** **[VERIFY: BLS historical series]**. The decline has many causes — global trade, deindustrialization, legal changes, employer resistance — but the result is a dramatic reduction in the organizational capacity of one of the largest historical solutions to the collective action problem facing workers.

### What this means for deliberation

Olson's argument is the structural reason a platform like this needs to exist at all. If diffuse interests organized themselves naturally, no coordination layer would be needed. The whole point of building deliberative infrastructure is to lower the cost of collective action for groups that the existing institutional landscape leaves underorganized — and to do so without recreating the problems (selective incentives that distort the mission, organizational capture, partisan drift) that Olson's solutions eventually run into.

## Article 1.4: Institutions, Path Dependence, and Why Change Is Slow

**Section 1: How Things Work**

Douglass North, who shared the Nobel Prize in economics in 1993, defined **institutions** as "the rules of the game in a society, or, more formally, the humanly devised constraints that shape human interaction." This definition is broader than the everyday use of the word. An institution, in North's sense, is not a building or an organization. It is any rule — formal or informal — that structures how people interact. The U.S. Constitution is an institution. So is the unwritten norm that senators do not personally insult one another on the floor. So is the practice of tipping in restaurants. All of these are humanly devised constraints that shape behavior in predictable ways.

North's framework distinguishes between **formal rules** (constitutions, statutes, regulations, contracts) and **informal norms** (customs, codes of conduct, conventions, expectations). Both matter, and they often interact in surprising ways. A formal rule that lacks normative backing — a law no one feels obligated to follow — produces selective enforcement and loss of legitimacy. An informal norm without formal codification can govern behavior for centuries until it is suddenly violated and there is no formal mechanism to restore it. The relative weight of formal rules and informal norms varies across societies and across periods within a society.

The most important concept in North's framework, for the purposes of understanding why institutional change is hard, is **path dependence**. The idea is that the choices available at any given moment are constrained by choices made earlier, sometimes much earlier. Once an institution is established and people have organized their lives around it, changing it becomes costly even if a different institution would be more efficient on the merits. The longer an institution persists, the more activity is built around it, and the higher the cost of change.

The classic illustration of path dependence is the **QWERTY keyboard**. The story goes that the QWERTY layout was designed in the 1870s for early mechanical typewriters with specific engineering constraints that no longer apply, that more efficient layouts (like Dvorak) exist, but that QWERTY persists because everyone learned to type on it and switching costs are too high. **[Contested]** This story has itself been challenged. Stan Liebowitz and Stephen Margolis published a sharp critique in 1990 arguing that the alleged efficiency advantages of Dvorak were exaggerated by the original studies, and that QWERTY's persistence may reflect roughly comparable performance rather than dramatic lock-in. The QWERTY example is still useful for illustrating the *concept* of path dependence, but the empirical claim about its inefficiency is not as clean as the story suggests.

A clearer example from American institutions is the **Senate filibuster**. The filibuster is not in the Constitution; it emerged from procedural choices made in the early 1800s, was rarely used through most of the nineteenth century, and gradually accumulated rules and norms that gave it its current form. At various points across the past century, majorities in the Senate have wanted to abolish or substantially weaken the filibuster, and yet it persists in some form. The reasons are several: senators in the minority value it because they may benefit from it later, senators in the majority face uncertainty about whether they will remain in the majority, and the procedural rules for changing Senate rules are themselves shaped by the filibuster. Each successive Senate inherits a structure that constrains its choices, even when the sitting majority would prefer a different structure on the merits.

A more recent example of formal rule change producing unexpected outcomes is the *Citizens United v. FEC* decision (2010), which removed restrictions on independent political expenditures by corporations and unions. The decision did not create the system of well-funded outside spending groups that followed; it created the legal space in which that system rapidly emerged. The new institutional landscape — Super PACs, dark-money 501(c)(4)s, coordinated independent expenditures — was not legislated into existence. It evolved as actors discovered what the new rules permitted and built organizations to exploit them. This is a useful illustration of how a single formal rule change can cascade into a much larger institutional reorganization that no one specifically designed.

How does institutional change actually happen, when path dependence makes the status quo so sticky? The historical institutionalist literature tends to identify a few patterns. **External shocks** — wars, economic crises, technological disruptions — can disrupt existing equilibria and create windows for reorganization. **Coalition shifts** can change which actors hold decisive power and reshape the rules in their favor. **Slow norm erosion** can hollow out informal constraints over decades until a single high-profile violation triggers a cascade. What is rare, in the historical record, is **rational redesign** — sitting down and replacing an existing institution with a better one through deliberate consensus. Most institutional change is partial, contested, and shaped by the prior structure even as it modifies it.

**North's framework is widely used but not without critics.** Some scholars argue that it underweights agency, power, and ideas relative to efficiency-based accounts of why institutions persist. Historical materialists argue that economic structures drive institutional change rather than the other way around. Constructivists argue that ideas and identities shape what counts as a "rule" in the first place. **[Contested]** These debates are active in political science and economic history, and the platform takes no position on them.

### What this means for deliberation

Anyone trying to change an institution should expect resistance disproportionate to the merits of the proposed change. Path dependence means that even bad institutions have stakeholders who have organized their lives around the existing rules, and that those stakeholders will fight to preserve the status quo. This is not corruption. It is the predictable consequence of how institutions accumulate constituencies. A deliberation platform that produces a clear consensus for change is producing useful information, but it is not, by itself, sufficient to overcome path dependence. The harder question is what kind of accumulated pressure can shift an institutional equilibrium, and over what timescale.

## Article 1.5: What Deliberative Democracy Actually Claims (and Doesn't)

**Section 1: How Things Work**

There are two broad ways of thinking about what democracy is for, and they imply very different institutional designs. The first, which political theorists call **aggregative democracy**, treats democracy as a procedure for combining preexisting individual preferences into a collective decision. People come to the political process with the preferences they already have, and the job of democratic institutions is to count those preferences fairly and produce an outcome — typically by majority rule — that reflects them. Voting is the central act. The legitimacy of the result depends on the fairness of the counting procedure.

The second view, **deliberative democracy**, treats democracy as a procedure for *transforming* preferences through reasoned exchange. On this view, the preferences people walk in with are partial, uninformed, often contradictory, and shaped by limited exposure to other perspectives. The job of democratic institutions is not just to count those preferences but to expose them to argument, evidence, and the perspectives of others, so that what gets counted at the end is more reflective and more informed than what was there at the start. Discussion is the central act. Voting may still happen, but it happens after a process designed to change what people are voting on.

The theoretical foundation for deliberative democracy is most often traced to the German philosopher Jürgen Habermas, whose *Theory of Communicative Action* (1981) developed the concept of the **ideal speech situation**. In Habermas's framing, an ideal deliberation has properties like equal access to participation, freedom from coercion, the ability of any participant to question any claim, and a shared commitment to arriving at conclusions through the force of better arguments rather than through power. No real deliberation meets this ideal. Habermas's claim is that the ideal serves as a regulative standard against which actual deliberations can be evaluated and improved.

This is, for some critics, exactly the problem. **[Contested]** The most influential critique within democratic theory came from Lynn Sanders, whose 1997 essay "Against Deliberation" argued that deliberative settings systematically privilege people who are comfortable with certain styles of speech — typically educated, white, male, articulate in the dominant idiom — and that the ideal of rational exchange masks the reproduction of existing social hierarchies in deliberative form. Sanders's critique does not refute deliberative theory but it forces a serious question: who gets heard in a deliberation, and on what terms?

The empirical question of whether deliberation actually changes minds — and in what direction — has been studied most extensively by James Fishkin and his collaborators at Stanford's Center for Deliberative Democracy. Fishkin's signature method is **Deliberative Polling**, which selects a random sample of citizens, surveys their views on an issue, brings them together for a structured deliberation with balanced briefing materials and access to experts, and surveys them again afterward. The methodology has been used in over a hundred studies in more than thirty countries.

Fishkin's headline finding is that participants change their minds on a substantial fraction of issues — by some measures, around **70% of policy questions show statistically measurable opinion change** — and that the direction of change is generally toward more nuanced, more informed, and more moderate positions. The effect is largest on questions where participants started with low information and where the briefing materials introduced facts they had not previously considered. **[Evidence mixed]** on how durable the changes are: some studies find that participants revert toward their original views after returning to their normal information environment, while others find more lasting effects. Fishkin himself has been careful about claims of universal generalizability, and the conditions of a Deliberative Poll — paid time off, balanced materials, expert access, professional facilitation — are expensive to replicate at scale.

The strongest real-world test of structured deliberation producing policy outcomes is **Ireland's Citizens' Assembly on the Eighth Amendment** (2016–2017). The Irish government convened a randomly selected body of ninety-nine citizens, who deliberated over several months with expert testimony from medical, legal, and ethical specialists representing a range of views on Ireland's constitutional ban on abortion. The assembly produced recommendations that informed the framing of a 2018 referendum, in which Irish voters approved repeal of the Eighth Amendment by a substantial margin. This is the strongest case of a deliberative process producing a major policy change in a democratic system. **A critical caveat:** the Irish process worked because the government chose to convene it and committed in advance to act on its recommendations. Where government does not provide that commitment, deliberation produces recommendations without enforcement mechanisms.

A useful contrast is the **British Columbia Citizens' Assembly on Electoral Reform** (2004), which spent nearly a year deliberating on whether and how to change British Columbia's electoral system. The assembly recommended a single transferable vote system, which was then put to provincial referendum. The proposal received 57.7% support — short of the 60% supermajority threshold the government had set — and was not adopted. **[VERIFY: BC referendum vote share and threshold]** The assembly worked exactly as designed; the institutional framework around it determined that the work did not translate into change.

### What this means for deliberation

Deliberative democracy is not a claim that talking solves everything. It is a more modest empirical claim: under certain structured conditions, ordinary people exposed to evidence and to other perspectives shift their positions in measurable ways, often toward greater nuance. Whether those shifts translate into policy depends on whether the deliberation is connected to a decision process that takes its results seriously. A deliberation disconnected from any such process produces an interesting artifact and not much more. This platform is built on the empirical premise that deliberation can change minds, and on the strategic premise that the harder question is what to do with the result.

## Article 1.6: Democratic Backsliding — What the Evidence Shows

**Section 1: How Things Work**

The phrase "democratic backsliding" entered the academic literature roughly two decades ago to describe a pattern that did not fit older models of democratic collapse. The classic image of a democracy ending was the **coup**: tanks in the streets, the legislature dissolved, an elected government replaced by a military junta. This still happens in some places, but it has become rarer. What scholars began noticing was a different pattern: democratically elected leaders using legal and constitutional means to gradually concentrate power, weaken checks on the executive, marginalize opposition, and erode the conditions that make future democratic competition meaningful.

The political scientists Steven Levitsky and Daniel Ziblatt, in *How Democracies Die* (2018), describe this pattern as **executive aggrandizement**, a term they borrow from earlier scholarship. The defining features are a slow accumulation of power within the executive at the expense of horizontal accountability institutions — courts, independent agencies, the legislature, the press — combined with the closing of civic space through legal harassment of opposition, restrictions on media, and the cultivation of partisan loyalty among officials whose role is supposed to be neutral. None of these moves looks like a coup. Each one, taken individually, is defensible on some legal or political ground. Taken together, they hollow out the institutional substance of democratic competition while leaving its formal procedures intact.

The largest systematic effort to measure these trends globally is the **V-Dem Institute** at the University of Gothenburg, which publishes annual democracy reports based on expert coding of dozens of indicators across nearly every country. V-Dem distinguishes between several dimensions of democracy — electoral, liberal, participatory, deliberative, egalitarian — and tracks each over time. Their most recent reports document a decade-long global trend in which the number of countries classified as autocratizing has exceeded the number classified as democratizing, with the affected population concentrated in a few large states. **[VERIFY: V-Dem most recent annual figures, exact framing]** The headline figures change each year and should be checked against the current report rather than cited from memory.

V-Dem's methodology has known limitations. **[Contested]** The data depend on expert coders making judgment calls about indicators that do not have purely objective measures. Different coders can reach different conclusions on borderline cases. V-Dem addresses this through aggregation and statistical modeling, but the underlying inputs are not raw observations of the kind that, say, GDP figures attempt to be. This does not invalidate the data; it means the data should be treated as informed expert judgment rather than as direct measurement. Critics point out that this introduces potential biases that purely formal measures would not have. Defenders point out that purely formal measures (Did an election happen? Was the result accepted?) miss exactly the slow erosion of substance that the concept of backsliding is trying to capture.

The Levitsky and Ziblatt framework has itself been criticized. **[Contested]** Their account focuses on elite behavior — what political leaders do, what norms they violate, what guardrails fail — and pays less attention to the structural and economic forces that may be driving the erosion in the first place. Daron Acemoglu and James Robinson, in *The Narrow Corridor* (2019), offer a complementary structural account that emphasizes the long historical process by which states and societies develop the capacity to constrain each other, and the conditions under which that balance can be lost. The two accounts are not exclusive — elite behavior and structural conditions both matter — but they point to different leverage points for anyone trying to resist democratic erosion.

The American case is genuinely contested among political scientists. **[Contested]** Some scholars argue that the United States has experienced clear democratic backsliding by the technical criteria used in the comparative literature: declining trust in elections, the erosion of norms around peaceful transfer of power, the politicization of nominally neutral institutions, the increasing willingness of partisan actors to use legal tools against political opponents. Others argue that what the United States is experiencing is something distinct: extreme polarization, legislative dysfunction, and the displacement of policymaking from the legislature toward the executive and the judiciary, but not backsliding in the sense the term is used about Hungary or Venezuela. These are not the same diagnosis and they imply somewhat different responses. **[We don't know]** which framing will look correct in retrospect.

What is less contested is that the U.S. legislative branch has lost relative capacity over recent decades. Congressional staff levels (committee staff in particular) have not kept pace with the complexity of the issues they oversee. Major policy is increasingly made through executive orders, agency rulemaking, and judicial interpretation rather than through the passage of new legislation. This is not by itself democratic backsliding, but it does mean that the institution most directly accountable to voters — the legislature — has less effective control over policy outcomes than it did a generation ago. The platform's focus on state legislative engagement reflects a judgment that state legislatures, while imperfect, remain a more tractable point of entry than the federal level for the kinds of cross-cutting issues described in the platform's problem statement.

### What this means for deliberation

Whatever language one uses for the current moment, the trend lines in legislative capacity, institutional trust, and elite norm-following do not look healthy. A platform that aims to influence policy through deliberation has to take seriously the possibility that the institutions it is trying to influence are themselves in a slow process of change. This does not make the work less important. It does mean that "produce a good deliberation and the legislature will act on it" is not a complete theory, and that the platform's design has to anticipate the possibility of working in an environment where formal democratic procedures continue while their substance erodes.

## Article 1.7: What a 501(c)(4) Is and Isn't

**Section 1: How Things Work**

The U.S. tax code carves out dozens of categories of organizations that are exempt from federal income tax. The two most relevant to civic and political activity are **501(c)(3)** and **501(c)(4)**, named after the subsections of Internal Revenue Code §501 that define them. They are often confused. The differences matter.

A **501(c)(3)** is a charitable, educational, religious, scientific, or literary organization. Donations to a (c)(3) are tax-deductible for the donor, which is the reason most people associate (c)(3) status with the word "nonprofit." In exchange for the deduction, (c)(3)s face the strictest restrictions on political activity. They may not engage in any partisan electioneering — no endorsing candidates, no opposing candidates, no contributions to campaigns, no coordinated independent expenditures. They may engage in some lobbying, but only as an "insubstantial" portion of their activities; what counts as substantial is partly defined by an elective expenditure test under Section 501(h) and partly by the IRS's older "substantial part" test. Crossing the line risks loss of tax-exempt status.

A **501(c)(4)** is a "social welfare organization." The category is broader and the restrictions are looser. A (c)(4) may engage in **unlimited lobbying** as long as the lobbying is related to its social welfare purpose. It may engage in some political activity — including supporting or opposing candidates — but only as a secondary activity, not as its primary purpose. The trade-off is that donations to a (c)(4) are **not tax-deductible** for the donor. This is the reason many advocacy organizations operate as a (c)(4), or as a paired (c)(3) and (c)(4) where the (c)(3) handles education and research and the (c)(4) handles lobbying and limited electoral activity.

**Political action committees (PACs)** are a separate category, regulated primarily by the Federal Election Commission rather than the IRS. A traditional PAC raises money from individuals (subject to contribution limits) and gives that money to candidates (also subject to contribution limits). A **Super PAC**, which became possible after the 2010 *Citizens United* decision and the related *SpeakNow* ruling, may raise unlimited sums from individuals, corporations, and unions, and spend unlimited sums on independent political expenditures, but may not coordinate those expenditures with candidates or their campaigns. Super PACs are required to disclose their donors. **[VERIFY: SpeakNow citation and the precise scope of Super PAC disclosure rules]**

What is sometimes called "dark money" is donations to 501(c)(4)s that are then used for political purposes. Because (c)(4)s are not required to disclose their donors to the public — only to the IRS, on a confidential schedule — money can flow into the political system through a (c)(4) without the donors being publicly identified. This has been controversial. Critics argue that it allows wealthy donors to fund political campaigns anonymously through legal arbitrage. Defenders argue that donor privacy is itself a First Amendment value and that disclosure requirements can chill legitimate political association, particularly for unpopular causes. Both views have some support in case law, and the balance between them has shifted over time.

The hardest part of (c)(4) compliance is the **"primary purpose" test**. The statute and IRS regulations say that a 501(c)(4) must be operated *primarily* for the promotion of social welfare, and that political campaign intervention cannot be its primary activity. What "primarily" means in practice is genuinely ambiguous. The IRS has historically used something like a 49%/51% threshold — political activity must be less than half of total activities — but the agency has also said that no single bright line applies, and enforcement has been inconsistent. **[Contested]** Several high-profile (c)(4)s have engaged in extensive political activity without losing their status. A major IRS enforcement push in the early 2010s, focused on Tea Party–affiliated (c)(4)s, became politically explosive and was followed by what most observers describe as a period of retreat from active enforcement of the primary purpose test. The current state of enforcement is unclear.

For an organization considering (c)(4) status, the practical implications are several. **First**, donations are not tax-deductible, which limits the donor base relative to a (c)(3). **Second**, the organization may lobby without limit, which is essential for any group whose theory of change involves direct legislative engagement. **Third**, the organization may engage in some electoral activity but cannot make electoral activity its primary purpose without losing its tax-exempt status — and the line is fuzzy enough that any significant electoral activity invites scrutiny. **Fourth**, the organization must file Form 990 annually, which becomes public, but it does not need to disclose its donors publicly. **Fifth**, the organization must register as a charitable solicitor in any state where it raises funds from residents — a tedious but mandatory compliance burden in roughly forty states.

**This article is descriptive, not legal advice.** The actual legal landscape for any specific organization depends on its activities, its jurisdiction, the specifics of its donor relationships, and changes in IRS enforcement that the IRS itself does not always announce in advance. Any organization considering 501(c)(4) status should retain qualified nonprofit and election law counsel before incorporating, before accepting major contributions, and before engaging in any activity that could be characterized as electoral. The cost of getting this wrong includes loss of tax-exempt status, personal liability for officers, and FEC enforcement actions.

### What this means for deliberation

The platform's choice of legal structure shapes what it can and cannot do, and any participant should know roughly what those constraints are. A 501(c)(4) can lobby a state legislature without limit, which is what makes the platform's theory of change legally possible. It cannot become primarily an electoral organization without losing its status. The trade-off — no tax deduction for donors, in exchange for the ability to lobby freely — is one the platform's founders made deliberately. It is reversible if circumstances change, but the current structure is what it is, and the legal constraints that come with it are real.

## Article 1.8: How Markets Fail (and Why That Doesn't Mean Markets Are Bad)

**Section 1: How Things Work**

Economists use the term **market failure** in a specific and narrow technical sense. A market failure is a situation in which the conditions required for a competitive market to produce an efficient outcome are not met, where "efficient" has a precise meaning: an allocation of resources in which it is not possible to make any one person better off without making someone else worse off. This is called **Pareto efficiency**, after the Italian economist Vilfredo Pareto, and it is the standard against which mainstream economics evaluates whether a market is "working."

The point of explaining this carefully is that "market failure" in the technical sense is not a synonym for "the market produced a result I don't like." Markets routinely produce outcomes that are technically efficient but morally objectionable, or technically inefficient but politically acceptable. The technical concept is narrower than the everyday use of the term and more useful as a result.

The standard taxonomy recognizes four major categories of market failure.

**Public goods** are goods that are non-excludable (you cannot prevent people from benefiting from them) and non-rival (one person's consumption does not reduce another's). National defense is the classic example: if a country defends its borders, it defends everyone within them, regardless of who paid. Because individuals cannot be excluded from the benefit, they have a rational incentive to free-ride on others' contributions, and the good is under-produced relative to what people would actually want if they could be made to pay. This is the same logic as Olson's collective action problem, applied to economics.

**Externalities** are costs or benefits of an activity that fall on third parties not involved in the transaction. The classic negative externality is pollution: a factory producing widgets imposes air-quality costs on downwind residents who are not party to the buying or selling of widgets. The factory's private cost is lower than the social cost, so it produces more widgets than it would if it had to compensate the downwind residents. The classic positive externality is vaccination: when you get vaccinated, you reduce disease transmission to others, but the benefit to those others does not show up in your private decision about whether to get vaccinated. As a result, the market under-produces vaccinations relative to the social optimum.

**Information asymmetry** describes situations in which one party to a transaction has materially better information than the other. The seller of a used car knows things about the car that the buyer cannot easily verify; the buyer's rational response is to discount the offer price, which drives down prices for sellers of good cars, who eventually leave the market — the famous "lemons" problem analyzed by George Akerlof in 1970. Healthcare is a much-studied case: patients cannot easily evaluate the quality of medical services, insurance companies cannot easily evaluate the risk profile of applicants, and the resulting equilibria deviate sharply from the textbook competitive market.

**Natural monopoly** describes situations in which the cost structure of an industry makes it more efficient for a single firm to serve the entire market than for multiple firms to compete. Industries with very high fixed costs and very low marginal costs — water utilities, electricity grids, last-mile broadband infrastructure — tend toward natural monopoly because duplicating the infrastructure is wasteful. The challenge is that a single firm with no competition has no market discipline on pricing or quality, which is why natural monopolies tend to be either publicly owned or heavily regulated.

There is a symmetric concept called **government failure**, developed in the public choice tradition associated with economists like James Buchanan, Gordon Tullock, and George Stigler. Government failure refers to situations in which government intervention produces outcomes that are themselves inefficient — sometimes more inefficient than the market failure the intervention was meant to correct. The mechanisms include regulatory capture (regulators come to identify with the interests they regulate), rent-seeking (organized groups lobby for rules that benefit them at others' expense), bureaucratic inefficiency, and the simple fact that public officials have their own interests and limited information just as market actors do.

George Stigler's "The Theory of Economic Regulation" (1971) is the foundational paper on **regulatory capture**, the pattern in which regulators come to serve the interests of the industry they regulate rather than the public. Stigler's argument was that regulation, viewed as a product, is acquired by an industry and operated for its benefit — not through corruption but through the structural fact that the industry has more resources, more information, and more sustained interest in the outcomes than the diffuse public. The capture concept applies broadly and is not unique to any particular political tradition.

The reason both market failure and government failure matter is that the policy question is almost always about **the relative magnitude of each in a specific context**, not a categorical choice between markets and government. A pure-market position requires denying that externalities, public goods, information asymmetries, and natural monopolies are real or significant. A pure-government position requires denying that regulatory capture, bureaucratic inefficiency, and unintended consequences are real or significant. Neither extreme survives serious examination.

Concrete examples across domains:

**Drug pricing** involves a deliberate policy trade-off. Patent monopoly is an intentional government grant of temporary market power to pharmaceutical innovators, designed to create incentives for the costly process of drug development. The trade-off is between **static efficiency** (lower prices and broader access for existing drugs) and **dynamic efficiency** (incentives to develop new drugs). The tradeoff is real. The optimal balance is genuinely uncertain and depends on empirical questions — how responsive R&D investment is to expected returns, how much of the price differential funds genuine innovation versus marketing, how to count the welfare gains from drugs that would not otherwise exist — that economists do not fully agree on.

**Environmental regulation** involves the externality case for carbon pricing. The economic case that emitting carbon imposes uncompensated costs on others is essentially uncontested among mainstream economists. The optimal level of the carbon price — and whether it should be implemented as a tax, a cap-and-trade system, or direct regulation — is contested and depends on empirical estimates of the social cost of carbon, the elasticity of emissions to price, and political feasibility considerations.

**Broadband access** illustrates the natural monopoly problem in last-mile infrastructure (the wires or fiber connecting individual homes to a network) combined with potential competition in the services delivered over that infrastructure. Many countries have addressed this by separating the two layers — regulated or publicly owned infrastructure, competitive service provision over it. The U.S. has not, and the resulting market structure is one of the most studied and contested in telecommunications policy.

**Housing** involves zoning as a government-created restriction on supply that interacts with natural geographic constraints. Both matter. In some markets, zoning is the dominant constraint; in others, geography is. Disentangling them is empirically difficult and the policy implications vary by market.

**This article presents the mainstream economics consensus, which is itself contested.** **[Contested]** Heterodox economic traditions — post-Keynesian, institutionalist, Modern Monetary Theory, ecological economics — challenge core elements of the framework presented here, including the meaningfulness of Pareto efficiency as a normative standard, the assumption that market exchange is the default against which interventions are measured, and the treatment of money and finance in standard models. These debates are real and ongoing in academic economics. The platform takes no position on them and presents the mainstream framework with its known limitations noted, because the mainstream framework is the language in which most policy debates are conducted and participants benefit from understanding it on its own terms.

### What this means for deliberation

Most policy disagreements that look like fights over values turn out, on closer examination, to involve disagreements about empirical magnitudes — how big the externality is, how responsive supply is to price, how much of the gap between rich and poor reflects market processes versus institutional choices. Recognizing this does not resolve the disagreements, but it can change what kind of evidence would be relevant to resolving them. A deliberation that distinguishes between empirical disagreements and values disagreements is doing something different — and in the long run more productive — than a deliberation in which both kinds of question get treated identically.

## Article 2.1: The Coordination Gap — Our Starting Diagnosis

**Section 2: Why This Platform Exists**

This is our diagnosis. We believe the evidence supports it. We also believe it can be falsified, and we have tried to specify how. What follows is the founders' view of why this platform exists — not a neutral summary.

We start from an observation that we think is hard to dispute: in the United States, on a number of issues, large and stable majorities of the public hold a policy view that the legislative system has not produced. This is not an isolated phenomenon. It is a recurring pattern across decades and across policy domains. The most-studied case is prescription drug pricing, where polling has shown roughly 80–90% support for allowing Medicare to negotiate drug prices for many years before the partial action in the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022. **[VERIFY: Pew/KFF polling figures and the most recent IRA-related provisions]** The pharmaceutical and health products industry spent roughly **$374 million on federal lobbying in 2023**, the largest of any sector, throughout this period.

The drug pricing case is the most dramatic but it is not unique. Universal background checks for firearm purchases have polled at over 80% support for more than a decade without federal legislation. **[VERIFY: Pew Research most recent figures on background checks]** Paid family leave has consistently shown majority support in polling and the United States remains, by OECD measures, the only high-income country without a national paid leave policy. **[VERIFY: OECD database on family leave provisions]** Public broadband options, in states where they have been seriously proposed, have repeatedly faced organized telecommunications industry opposition that has defeated them despite favorable public opinion. The pattern recurs.

We do not think this is because elected officials are corrupt in the legal sense. Most are not. We think it is because the existing institutional system fails at a different point on each policy issue, and the failures compound.

**Elections** fail because a vote for a candidate is a bundled signal across hundreds of issues. Christopher Achen and Larry Bartels, in *Democracy for Realists* (2016), document at length the empirical case that voting behavior is driven much more by social identity and partisan loyalty than by issue positions. We find their evidence persuasive. It does not mean voters are stupid. It means that elections do a different job than the folk theory of democracy assigns to them: they select leaders, and they signal coalition membership, but they do not faithfully transmit policy preferences.

**Advocacy organizations** fail in a different way. The most common failure is the slow drift of organizational priorities away from member preferences and toward whatever sustains the organization itself — donor relationships, staff career incentives, partisan alignment in moments of crisis. MoveOn, founded as a nonpartisan accountability mechanism in 1998, became a Democratic-aligned organization within a decade because its most engaged donors and activists were Democrats. We do not think MoveOn's leadership did anything wrong. We think the structural pressures on a membership organization with no mechanism for member governance produce that drift reliably.

**Polling** fails because it measures preferences without converting them into anything. A poll showing 80% support for a policy is information. It is not pressure. Politicians have many reasons to discount poll results — the salience problem (do voters care enough to vote on this issue?), the intensity problem (are the supporters as motivated as the opponents?), the credibility problem (is this poll methodologically sound or sponsored by an interested party?). A polling result that does not come attached to organized people is, from a legislator's perspective, a piece of background information rather than a constraint on action.

**Social media** fails because it is not optimized for deliberation or coordination. The major platforms are advertising businesses, and the metric they maximize is engagement — time on platform, content interaction, ad impressions. Outrage, identity signaling, and tribal conflict produce engagement. Reasoned deliberation does not. We do not think this is a bug to be fixed; we think it is the structural consequence of the underlying business model. A platform whose revenue depends on engagement cannot sustainably serve coordination because coordination is engagement-poor.

So far this is the structural story. Our problem statement adds two further forces that we think make the gap durable.

The first is **intensity asymmetry**, which we discussed at length in Article 1.3. Olson's argument — that small groups with concentrated stakes organize easily, while large groups with diffuse stakes do not — is, we think, the single most important structural fact about American politics on the issues this platform targets. It explains why pharmaceutical companies sustain a $374 million annual lobbying operation while patients paying high drug prices do not. The patients have a larger collective stake. They cannot organize as effectively.

The second is **information asymmetry as a force multiplier**. Organized economic interests do not just outspend citizen coalitions in lobbying; they shape the information environment in which preferences are formed. Industry-funded research, friendly think tanks, hired experts, and sustained communications campaigns operate over decades to shape what counts as conventional wisdom on a given issue. Citizens whose preferences are formed in this information environment may be making "free" choices in some sense, but the choices have been shaped by parties with concentrated interests and substantial resources. We do not think this means citizen preferences are illegitimate. We think it means that any deliberation platform has to take seriously the question of what information participants are deliberating on.

Putting all of this together, here is our claim:

**No persistent, self-initiating institutional mechanism exists for converting majority-but-moderate-intensity citizen preferences on broad, cross-cutting policy issues into concentrated political force capable of overcoming the organized opposition of concentrated economic interests — when those interests have systematically shaped both the legislative environment and the information environment in which citizen preferences are formed.**

The gap is most severe at the intersection of four conditions:
- The issue is broad and cross-cutting, affecting a heterogeneous population with no shared identity that would sustain single-issue mobilization.
- Citizen preference is convergent (super-majority support) but not existential — no individual will vote on this single issue alone.
- Opposing economic interests are concentrated, with high per-actor stakes and organized lobbying capacity.
- The legislative institution with formal authority to act is itself subject to the same organizational pressures that produce the gap.

This is a narrower claim than "the political system is broken." We think narrower claims are more useful because they can be falsified.

**How would we know we are wrong?** Two ways.

We would be wrong if, on issues satisfying all four conditions, existing mechanisms reliably produced legislative outcomes aligned with citizen super-majority preferences within one or two legislative sessions. The available evidence on drug pricing, background checks, and several other cases does not support this, but the claim is empirical rather than definitional, and we are open to being shown otherwise.

We would also be wrong if an existing mechanism — a reformed advocacy organization, a citizens' assembly, a union coalition — has solved the problem in at least one instance satisfying all four conditions. The closest cases we know of either fail one of the conditions (AARP's success on Social Security depends on a single demographic with a single existential issue, failing the "broad and cross-cutting" condition) or depend on government cooperation that is precisely what the captured-legislature condition rules out (Ireland's Citizens' Assembly worked because the Irish government chose to act on it). If a clean counterexample exists, we want to know about it.

**A note on what this claim does not include.** Our problem statement deliberately does not say that all political participants want policy outcomes. We know — and we think the evidence is clear — that many political participants want belonging, identity expression, and the satisfactions of conflict and tribal affiliation. This is not a moral failing. It is a real feature of how humans engage with politics. We do not think it falsifies the coordination gap. We think it identifies a design constraint: any platform that aims to serve outcome-oriented coordination must also offer enough of what people actually come to politics for, or it will have no participants. We are still working out how to do that. We do not pretend it is solved.

### What this means for participation

If you are reading this and considering whether to participate, here is the honest summary: we think there is a real institutional gap, we think this platform is one possible response to it, and we are not sure it will work. We have tried to be specific about what we are claiming so that you can evaluate it yourself rather than taking our word.

## Article 2.2: What We're Building and Why We Think It Could Work

**Section 2: Why This Platform Exists**

The previous article described the problem as we see it. This article describes what we're building in response and why we think it has some chance of working. We are going to be specific, and where we are uncertain we will say so.

The platform's central asset, if we succeed, is a category of object that does not currently exist in American politics: a **documented, verified, deliberation-produced policy position from identified constituents in specific legislative districts**. We think this object is qualitatively different from a poll or a petition, and we think the difference is what gives the platform any leverage at all.

A poll is a statistical abstraction. When a legislator hears that "67% of voters in our state favor X," they hear a number that is not attached to any specific person and that they cannot verify. A petition is a list of names, but petitions have been so devalued by the ease of digital signing that legislators rationally treat them as low-information signals. Neither produces a political cost to ignoring, because the people behind the number cannot be identified, contacted, or held accountable for the position they expressed.

A platform position is different in four ways. **It is verified**: the people behind it are identifiable residents of specific districts, not anonymous accounts. **It is deliberated**: the position emerged from a structured process in which participants engaged with the actual text of a bill and considered the arguments for and against it, not from a single click on a petition. **It is specific**: it is a position on a particular bill with particular provisions, not a general expression of preference. **It is monitored**: the same participants who produced the position are watching how their legislators vote on it, and the platform publishes a public record of the alignment.

Our reference case for the value of accountability infrastructure is the National Rifle Association. We do not endorse the NRA's policy positions. We do think their accountability model is the most effective example we have of a small membership organization sustaining political influence disproportionate to its numbers, and the mechanism is worth understanding regardless of how one feels about the politics. The NRA grades legislators A through F on gun-related votes. The grades are public, persistent, searchable, and trusted by their members. Members vote on gun rights as a primary issue in primary elections, and primary elections are where the NRA's leverage is concentrated. A legislator who receives an F from the NRA faces a real cost: they can be challenged in a primary by a candidate with NRA backing and a base of single-issue voters who actually show up to vote on the basis of that score.

We are not the NRA. We do not have decades of credibility, a five-million-member base, or a constituency willing to vote on a single issue. What we have is the architecture for building, slowly, the same kind of accountability infrastructure on issues that currently lack it. The MVP is an attempt to test whether the first link in this chain — verified deliberation producing a specific position that a professional advocate delivers to legislators — actually changes how legislators respond. If it does, the rest of the chain becomes possible to build. If it does not, the model is wrong and we will need to revise it.

Here is our theory of change in plain language, with the explicit acknowledgment that each step contains an assumption that can fail:

1. We recruit verified residents of a target state to deliberate on a specific pending bill using the platform's phase-gated process. **Assumption**: enough people will engage at sufficient depth to produce a substantive outcome.

2. The deliberation produces a documented position with verified participant signatures and district affiliations. **Assumption**: deliberation actually converges on a position rather than fracturing along culture-war lines.

3. A professional state lobbyist delivers the position to target legislators, framing it explicitly as a verified-constituent product rather than an advocacy position. **Assumption**: the framing differentiates the document from standard advocacy materials in a way legislators notice.

4. The platform publishes a scorecard tracking legislator votes against the deliberated position. **Assumption**: the scorecard is findable, persistent, and at least minimally credible.

5. Legislators face a novel accountability problem — identifiable constituents with documented positions who are actively monitoring the vote — and at least some of them weigh this differently than they would weigh a standard advocacy ask.

6. Over time and over multiple deliberations, the scorecard accumulates credibility and constituent reach. The reputational cost of low scores compounds.

7. Eventually, legislators begin to consult platform positions proactively rather than reacting to them after the fact.

Steps 1 through 4 are the MVP. Steps 5 through 7 are the scaling theory, and we want to be very clear that they are not validated. If the MVP fails to demonstrate that step 4 produces any measurable response from step 5, the rest of the theory is wrong and we will need to either revise it or stop.

**Here is what we cannot do at MVP scale.** We cannot, with five hundred or two thousand verified participants in one state, generate enough electoral pressure to change the behavior of a legislator who is genuinely captured by an opposing economic interest. The first deliberation will not change a major piece of legislation by itself. The first scorecard rating will not end anyone's career. The MVP is a credibility-building exercise and a hypothesis test, not a leverage instrument. We are honest about this with participants because the alternative — implying that one cycle will produce a major policy win — would be the same metric-gaming failure mode that destroyed Change.org's value as a coordination tool.

**Here is what we would need to reach to actually matter.** To generate the kind of pressure that can shift legislative behavior on major issues, we would need to reach a scale where verified platform participants in competitive districts are numerous enough that legislators consider their preferences as part of routine vote calculations. We estimate this is somewhere in the range of tens of thousands of active participants per state, concentrated in marginal districts, with several years of credibility-building behind the scorecard. None of this is achievable in the MVP or in the first few years after it. If the MVP works, the path to scale is long. If the MVP does not work, there is no path.

**What we don't know.** We do not know whether deliberation at the scale we are trying produces qualitatively different outputs from polls. We do not know whether legislators will respond differently to verified-constituent positions than to standard advocacy. We do not know whether the platform can sustain participant engagement across multiple deliberation cycles without optimizing for the engagement-driven dynamics we are trying to avoid. We do not know whether professional lobbyists can effectively represent a deliberation-derived position without distorting it. These are open empirical questions. The MVP is designed to start producing answers.

### What this means for participation

If you participate in an early deliberation, you are participating in an experiment whose outcome we do not know in advance. The point of the experiment is to test the theory described above. If the theory is right, you are helping build the first instance of an institution that we think is missing from American politics. If the theory is wrong, you are helping us learn that, which is also valuable but is not what we are hoping for. We think the honest framing is the right one.

## Article 2.3: Our Intellectual Influences

**Section 2: Why This Platform Exists**

A platform like this does not get designed in a vacuum. The choices we have made reflect specific intellectual influences, and we think it is more honest to name them than to present our choices as if they emerged from neutral analysis. Here are the thinkers and frameworks that have most directly shaped what we are building and why.

**Elinor Ostrom** is the single largest influence on our governance design. Ostrom's *Governing the Commons* (1990) documented dozens of cases in which communities successfully managed shared resources — fisheries, irrigation systems, forests, grazing lands — without either privatizing the resource or relying on external state coercion. From these cases she extracted eight design principles for long-enduring commons institutions: clearly defined boundaries, locally adapted rules, collective-choice arrangements that involve those affected, monitoring by members or accountable to members, graduated sanctions, accessible conflict resolution mechanisms, recognition of the right to organize, and (for systems embedded in larger systems) nested enterprises. These principles are not a recipe — they describe the conditions under which the cases she studied succeeded, not a formula that guarantees success elsewhere. But they are the closest thing we have to a tested framework for designing self-governing institutions, and we have used them to constrain our own choices. The phase-gated deliberation process, the structured signal system, the public audit log, the tiered identity model, and the explicit conflict resolution paths in the governance charter all map to one or more of Ostrom's principles. Where we have departed from them, we have tried to be explicit about why.

**James Fishkin** has shaped how we think about deliberation specifically. We discussed the empirical findings of Deliberative Polling in Article 1.5; what matters here is the interpretive claim that runs through Fishkin's work. He argues that the ordinary citizen's policy preferences, as measured by standard polling, are partial and uninformed not because citizens are deficient but because they are operating under conditions — limited time, limited access to balanced information, limited exposure to other perspectives — that no one would design if they were trying to elicit considered judgments. Under different conditions, the same citizens produce more nuanced and more reflective positions. This is the empirical foundation for our claim that deliberation is qualitatively different from preference aggregation and that it justifies the additional process cost it imposes. We are aware that the Fishkin findings are not unlimited and that the conditions are expensive to replicate. We think the qualitative insight survives those caveats.

**Christopher Achen and Larry Bartels** have shaped what we *don't* expect from elections. *Democracy for Realists* (2016) is the most rigorous case we know that voting behavior is primarily driven by social identity and partisan loyalty rather than by issue positions. We find their evidence persuasive. The implication is uncomfortable: it means that "vote them out" is a much weaker mechanism for converting policy preferences into outcomes than the folk theory of democracy assumes, because most votes are not really about issues. This sharpens rather than undermines our purpose. If elections were the efficient preference-aggregation mechanism the folk theory describes, this platform would not be needed. The Achen and Bartels result is a load-bearing piece of our diagnosis.

**Mancur Olson** sets the design challenge. *The Logic of Collective Action* (1965) explains why the diffuse interests we are trying to organize do not organize themselves, and why selective incentives — private benefits offered to members — have historically been the workaround. The challenge for us is that selective incentives create their own distortions: they shift the organization's incentive structure toward retaining members for the private benefits rather than serving the political mission, which is one of the mechanisms behind the slow drift of advocacy organizations away from their founding purposes. We don't have a clean solution to this. We think the right response is to be honest about the tension and to design with an eye toward keeping the political mission visible in the organization's metrics rather than letting member-retention metrics displace it.

**Albert Hirschman**, in *Exit, Voice, and Loyalty* (1970), provides the framework we use to think about how participants respond when the platform disappoints them. Hirschman observed that members of any organization have three options when things go wrong: exit (leaving), voice (protesting from within), or loyalty (staying despite problems, often as the precondition for exercising voice). The three are in tension: easy exit weakens voice (because the dissatisfied leave rather than fight to fix things), but the absence of exit makes voice less credible (because the organization knows members are stuck). For us, this maps onto our fork rights, our deliberation processes, and the community norms we are trying to develop. We want exit to be real — the codebase is open source, the data is portable, a fork is genuinely possible — because credible exit makes voice within the platform meaningful. We also want voice to be effective enough that exit is rarely necessary. The tension between these is permanent and we expect to be managing it indefinitely.

**The adversarial analysis** we subjected the concept to in early 2026 is also part of the intellectual lineage of the platform. The analysis took the form of a hostile critique of the project from the perspectives of institutional economics, political science, mechanism design, and civic tech history. It produced a numerical estimate that the probability of reaching durable institutional scale over a ten-year horizon is in the range of 5–15%. We took this seriously. We also decided to build the MVP anyway. Our reasoning is that 5–15% over a decade is not low enough to dismiss for a project whose downside cost is bounded — a learning vehicle, an open-source contribution, a small failed experiment — and whose upside is genuinely valuable if it works. We do not think long odds are a reason not to try. We do think they are a reason to plan honestly and to maintain genuine willingness to abandon the project if the early evidence does not support it.

There are influences we have not listed individually that are nonetheless visible in the design: Douglass North on path dependence and the difficulty of institutional change, James Scott on the dangers of high-modernist legibility schemes that ignore local knowledge, Habermas on the regulative ideal of unforced communication, and the broader public choice tradition on government failure and the reasons not to assume that any new institution is automatically an improvement on the existing landscape.

### What this means for participation

You do not need to agree with any of these thinkers to participate. We are listing them because we think the design choices the platform embodies came from somewhere, and because the people who participate in it should be able to evaluate those choices on their merits rather than treating them as neutral. If a design choice seems wrong to you, the right response is to make the argument — the platform's whole premise is that arguments matter and that they are worth having in public.

## Article 2.4: Failure Modes We're Trying to Avoid

**Section 2: Why This Platform Exists**

The history of civic technology is mostly a history of failure. Most platforms that set out to do something like what we are trying to do have collapsed, drifted, been captured, or quietly become irrelevant. We think the honest response to this is to study the specific ways previous projects failed and to be explicit about which failure modes we are trying to resist, what we are doing about it, and where our design is not enough on its own.

There are five canonical failure modes we think about most. We will describe each, name the case study, identify the structural force that produces it, and say what the platform's design does — and where it falls short.

**1. Partisan drift.** The case study is **MoveOn.org**, founded in 1998 as a nonpartisan accountability mechanism after the Clinton impeachment. Within a decade, MoveOn had become an effective Democratic-aligned organization. Its leaders did not, as far as we can tell, decide to abandon nonpartisanship as a goal. The drift happened because the most engaged donors and the most active members were Democrats, and the organization's economic survival depended on responding to its base. Once the base was identifiably partisan, the organization was effectively partisan too, regardless of its founding language. The structural force is straightforward: in a polarized political environment, the people most willing to spend time and money on political organizations are people with strong partisan identities, and any organization dependent on their participation will drift toward them.

We think this is the failure mode we are most likely to reproduce, because it is produced by participation patterns we cannot fully control. Our design responses are limited but specific. We have deliberately chosen issue framings (drug pricing, broadband access, occupational licensing) that do not map cleanly onto partisan coalitions. We have committed in our governance charter to a nonpartisan posture and to refusing endorsements of candidates of any party. We have built the audit log so that any drift in our actual behavior can be observed externally. None of this is sufficient on its own. If our active user base becomes substantially identified with one party, the platform will face the same pressures MoveOn did, and our resistance to them will depend on our willingness to make decisions our most engaged users do not like. We think this resistance is possible. We are not certain it is sustainable.

**2. Metric gaming.** The case study is **Change.org**, the petition platform. Change.org's growth strategy optimized for the metric it could measure — petition signatures — rather than the goal it claimed to pursue, which was policy change. The result was a platform that became extraordinarily good at producing petition signatures and roughly indifferent to whether the petitions accomplished anything. From a legislator's perspective, a Change.org petition is rationally treated as a low-information signal because the cost of signing is near zero and the platform's incentives are oriented toward creation rather than success. The structural force is **Goodhart's Law** — when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. Any platform that picks a quantitative engagement metric and optimizes for it will, over time, drift toward gaming that metric at the expense of whatever the metric was supposed to indicate.

Our design response is to explicitly *not* optimize for the metrics that produce engagement-driven failure. We do not have post upvotes. We do not have a ranked feed. We do not have engagement metrics for facilitators or participants. We do have phase gates that prevent fast-cycle interaction patterns. The risk is that we substitute different metrics — number of deliberations completed, number of legislators contacted, scorecard ratings published — that are equally susceptible to Goodhart drift. We are trying to use legislative outcome metrics where possible, even though they are slow and noisy, because outcome metrics are harder to game than engagement metrics. This is not a complete defense.

**3. Elite capture.** The case study is the general pattern, well-documented across the nonprofit sector, in which a small founding team or board makes the substantive decisions for an organization while the "membership" is functionally a donor base and a source of validation. MoveOn fits this pattern. Most major advocacy organizations fit this pattern, including organizations whose missions explicitly emphasize member governance. The structural force is that running an organization is full-time work, the people who do that work develop expertise and relationships that members cannot easily replicate, and decision authority concentrates in their hands by default. Formal governance structures that nominally include members are often ceremonial.

Our design response is to keep substantive decisions in the public deliberation process and the audit log rather than in private staff conversations. This is a much weaker defense than we would like. We do not have a mechanism that fully prevents the founding team from making decisions that participants would reject if asked. What we have is a commitment to making those decisions visible and to building toward governance structures that move authority toward the participants over time. We are aware that this is what every organization that drifts into elite capture also says about itself in its early years.

**4. Theory-of-change vacuum.** The case study is, again, Change.org, but it applies more broadly. The failure mode is producing aggregated preferences without specifying the mechanism by which those preferences are supposed to translate into political force. A platform that says "we will gather a million signatures and present them to Congress" has not specified anything resembling a theory of change. Why would Congress respond? What is the cost to a legislator of ignoring a million signatures? If the answer is "none," then the activity is symbolic, and over time even the participants notice. The structural force is that aggregating preferences is the easy part of the work, and specifying the mechanism of leverage is the hard part. Most platforms do the easy part and assume the hard part will take care of itself.

Our design response is to be specific about the leverage mechanism: verified district-level constituent positions delivered by a professional advocate, paired with a public scorecard that tracks legislator alignment over time. We discussed this in Article 2.2. The honest assessment is that the leverage mechanism is weak at MVP scale and depends on slow accumulation of credibility. We are trying not to make claims about what we cannot deliver, which is the failure mode we are explicitly trying to avoid.

**5. Government dependency.** The case study is **vTaiwan**, the digital deliberation platform that produced real legislative outcomes in Taiwan during the late 2010s. vTaiwan combined the Polis opinion-clustering tool with structured multi-phase deliberation and produced documented influence on Taiwanese legislation, most famously on Uber regulation. It is the strongest case we know of digital deliberation translating into policy. **And it required government cooperation.** The Taiwanese government chose to participate in the process and committed to acting on its results. Where governments do not provide that commitment, the same deliberation process produces high-quality artifacts with no destination.

This is a structural problem for our model, because the kinds of issues we are most interested in are exactly the kinds of issues where the legislative institution is the captured party. We cannot rely on government to convene the process or to act on its results. Our response is to build the platform to operate independently of government cooperation — recruiting participants directly, producing documents that legislators can read or ignore as they choose, and building the accountability infrastructure (the scorecard) to make ignoring the documents progressively more costly. Whether this is enough is the central open question of the project. We think it might be. We do not know.

A failure mode we have not listed but should mention briefly is **the identity expression problem**, which the adversarial analysis identified as the deepest threat to the platform's design. Many political participants want belonging and self-expression, not policy outcomes. A platform that offers structured deliberation and not much in the way of identity-expressive features may simply lose to platforms that offer the latter. We do not have a clean response to this. Our current bet is that there is a real and underserved population of people who want outcome-oriented coordination and who will tolerate the lack of engagement-driven features in exchange for a process they believe in. If that population does not exist, or does not exist in sufficient numbers, the platform will not work regardless of how well the rest of the design holds up.

### What this means for participation

You should know what we are afraid of. You should also know that our design responses to these fears are partial and that we expect to be revising them over time. If you participate and you see the platform drifting toward any of the failure modes described here, the right response is to say so — in a thread, in an email to us, in a public critique — because the audit log and the open governance are the mechanisms by which you, as a participant, can help us catch failures we are not catching ourselves.

## Article 3.1: Questions We Don't Answer For You

**Section 3: Open Questions**

This is the shortest section of the wiki and in some ways the most important. The platform exists to support deliberation, which means that on the questions deliberation is meant to address, the platform itself should take no position. This article exists to be explicit about what we will and will not do, and to flag a category distinction that deliberators have a recurring problem with: the difference between empirical questions and values questions.

An **empirical question** is a question about what is the case — a question that, in principle, evidence can resolve. How many people lack health insurance in Colorado? How does the price of insulin in the United States compare to its price in Canada? What is the carbon intensity of natural gas relative to coal generation? What was the employment effect of the most recent minimum wage increase in Seattle? These are not always easy to answer. Some have measurement difficulties. Some have multiple reasonable estimates. Some are answered differently by methodologically serious researchers using different assumptions. But they are the kind of question where, in principle, more data and better methods would converge on a single answer. The disagreement is about what is the case.

A **values question** is a question about what should be the case — a question that depends on weightings of legitimate but incommensurable goods. How much economic efficiency should we sacrifice to achieve a given level of equality? How should we balance the freedom of one person against the welfare of another? How much should we discount the well-being of people not yet born? These are not questions that more data can resolve, because reasonable people who agree on all the empirical facts can still disagree based on different value commitments. The disagreement is about what should be done given what is the case.

The distinction matters because **the platform has different obligations on the two kinds of question**.

On empirical questions where there is genuine scientific or economic consensus, the platform should not present the consensus as one of "two sides." Vaccine safety is not a values question. Climate attribution to human activity is not a values question. The administrative cost differential between single-payer and multi-payer healthcare systems is largely an empirical question with a substantial literature, and treating it as if it had no answer would mislead participants. Where consensus exists, the platform should reflect it. The hard part is recognizing when consensus actually exists versus when it has been manufactured by interested parties or eroded by motivated criticism, and we will not always get this right.

On values questions where reasonable people disagree, the platform should take no position. The appropriate role of government in private markets is a values question. The right tradeoff between individual liberty and collective welfare is a values question. How much present cost we should accept for future benefit is a values question. Whether the country should have universal healthcare, single-payer or otherwise, is partly a values question and partly an empirical question, and we should not pretend that it is purely one or the other.

There is a third category that is the hardest to handle: **questions that look like values disagreements but turn out to be partly empirical**. The optimal mechanism for setting drug prices is partly a values question (how should we weigh innovation against access?) and partly an empirical question (how responsive is innovation to expected returns? how much of current pricing funds genuine R&D versus marketing?). The minimum wage debate is partly a values question and partly an empirical question about how employment responds to wage floors at various levels in various labor markets. The efficiency of public versus private provision of specific services is partly a values question and partly an empirical question that depends heavily on the specific service and the specific institutional context.

When these mixed questions come up, the platform's epistemic commitment is to be as clear as we can about which parts are empirical and which parts are values, to flag where we are uncertain about the categorization, and to never present a genuine values question as if it had a scientifically correct answer that participants who disagree are simply ignorant of.

We will get this wrong sometimes. The categorization itself is contested in some cases — what looks like an empirical consensus to one observer can look like a manufactured consensus to another, and the boundary between empirical and values content is sometimes blurry. We will try to be transparent about our reasoning when we make a categorization call. We will publish disagreements about our categorizations rather than hiding them. We will treat any pattern of categorization decisions that consistently favors one political coalition as a warning sign that we are doing something wrong.

Here is the explicit commitment, which we want participants to be able to hold us to:

**If you find that this platform has pre-loaded answers to values questions — if you see us framing a contested values dispute as if one side were obviously right, or presenting a partisan policy preference as if it were the only reasonable conclusion from the evidence — that is a design failure. Tell us. We will treat it as a serious problem and try to fix it.**

The platform's legitimacy depends on participants being able to trust that the deliberative space is for their reasoning, not for ours. We are aware that this trust is hard to earn and easy to lose. The articles in Section 2 of this wiki are explicitly labeled as the founders' perspective, and we have tried to keep the articles in Section 1 cleanly descriptive. The deliberation tools themselves should be even more careful: they are where participants form positions that the platform then carries forward into the political system, and any thumb on the scale at that stage corrupts everything downstream.

### What this means for deliberation

The platform is not neutral about everything. It is not neutral about the value of deliberation, about the importance of accountability, about the design choices encoded in its architecture, or about the diagnosis described in Section 2. It is meant to be neutral about the substantive policy questions that participants are deliberating on, with the limited exception that it will reflect empirical consensus where empirical consensus exists. The line between these is hard to draw and we will not always draw it correctly. Participation includes the right to call us out when we draw it wrong.

## Article 3.2: Worked Examples — How the Platform Handles the Empirical/Values Distinction

**Section 3: Open Questions**

Article 3.1 established the platform's editorial commitment: distinguish empirical from values content, reflect consensus where it genuinely exists, take no position on contested values questions, and flag the mixed cases explicitly. This article gives worked examples of how that commitment plays out in practice on issues the platform is likely to deliberate. It is not a list of platform positions. It is a list of how we think about *framing* each issue for deliberation — what we will say is empirically settled, what we will say is contested, and what we will leave entirely to participants.

We are including this article because the editorial commitment in 3.1 is easy to state and hard to execute, and participants should be able to see examples of the execution rather than taking our word that we will do it correctly. The examples below are not exhaustive and they are not final. We expect to revise them as we encounter hard cases in actual deliberation.

### Example 1: Prescription Drug Pricing

**Empirical content we will reflect.** U.S. prescription drug prices are, on average, substantially higher than prices for the same drugs in other high-income countries. This is well documented across many studies and is not seriously contested in the health economics literature. The specific mechanism by which U.S. law prevents Medicare from directly negotiating most drug prices — the "non-interference clause" in Medicare Part D as originally passed in 2003, partially modified by the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 — is a matter of statutory fact and we will describe it as such. The administrative structure of pharmaceutical patents, exclusivity periods, and FDA approval pathways is also a matter of legal and regulatory fact.

**Empirical content that is genuinely contested.** The responsiveness of pharmaceutical R&D investment to expected returns is contested. Some studies find that reducing expected drug revenues would meaningfully reduce future innovation; others find that the link is weaker than industry arguments suggest. How much of the price differential between U.S. and other countries funds genuine novel research versus marketing, executive compensation, and returns to shareholders is contested. These are empirical questions where more or better data could in principle resolve the dispute, but where honest observers currently disagree. The platform will present the disagreement rather than picking a side.

**Values content that the platform will not take a position on.** How much innovation in future drugs is worth what level of access to current drugs — the core intergenerational and distributional tradeoff — is a values question. Whether access to life-extending medication is a right, a service, or a market commodity is a values question. How much the public should subsidize pharmaceutical research directly (e.g., through NIH) versus indirectly (through patent protection) depends partly on empirical estimates but ultimately on values about the role of government in innovation. The platform takes no position on these.

### Example 2: Zoning and Housing Supply

**Empirical content we will reflect.** Restrictive zoning in high-demand metropolitan areas reduces housing supply relative to what would exist under less restrictive rules. This is close to consensus in urban economics. The magnitude of the effect varies by market and is contested (see below), but the direction is not. The relationship between housing supply and prices, holding demand constant, is also close to consensus: more supply produces lower prices than would otherwise prevail.

**Empirical content that is genuinely contested.** How much of observed housing price variation across cities is driven by zoning versus geography, amenities, labor market strength, and other factors is contested and the estimates vary significantly across studies. The effect of specific upzoning policies on displacement of existing residents in the short run is contested — some studies find significant displacement effects, others find little. The interaction between housing supply and homelessness is contested in ways that matter for policy design. The platform will reflect the range of serious estimates rather than picking a single one.

**Values content that the platform will not take a position on.** How much weight to give existing residents' preferences (for neighborhood character, property values, school composition) versus prospective residents' interests (in access, affordability, opportunity) is a values question, and it is arguably the core values question in housing policy. Whether zoning decisions should be made at the neighborhood, city, or state level involves values about local control and democratic accountability. Whether housing should be treated primarily as a market commodity or as a basic need with government guarantees is a values question. The platform takes no position on these.

### Example 3: Minimum Wage

**Empirical content we will reflect.** The minimum wage in the United States is set by a combination of federal, state, and local law, and many states and cities have minimum wages higher than the federal floor. The statutory history is a matter of fact. The distributional profile of minimum-wage workers — who they are, what industries they work in, how much of household income they contribute — is well studied and we will describe it accurately.

**Empirical content that is genuinely contested.** The employment effect of minimum wage increases is one of the most extensively studied and most genuinely contested questions in labor economics. Serious researchers using serious methods have reached different conclusions. Card and Krueger's 1994 New Jersey study found minimal employment effects and triggered a long methodological debate. Subsequent work has found a range of results depending on the size of the increase, the tightness of the labor market, the specific methodology, and the industries studied. The honest summary is that moderate minimum wage increases in tight labor markets appear to have small employment effects, that large increases in slack labor markets may have larger effects, and that serious economists disagree on how to generalize from existing studies. The platform will reflect this range of findings rather than asserting a single answer.

**Values content that the platform will not take a position on.** How much weight to give workers who benefit from a higher minimum wage versus workers (or would-be workers) who might be displaced by it is a values question — and notably, it is one where even people who agree on the empirical estimates can reach different policy conclusions. Whether the minimum wage should be pegged to a cost-of-living index, to median wages, to productivity, or left as a political decision is a values question. Whether a higher minimum wage is the best policy instrument for raising low-wage incomes, or whether tools like the Earned Income Tax Credit are preferable, involves values and empirics both, and the platform takes no position on which combination is correct.

### Example 4: Broadband Access as a Public Utility

**Empirical content we will reflect.** Last-mile broadband infrastructure exhibits natural monopoly characteristics in many markets — the capital cost of building duplicate networks is high enough that competition is limited in most places. This is not seriously contested. The current structure of U.S. broadband provision, the specific regulatory framework, and the variation in prices and speeds across markets are all matters of documented fact.

**Empirical content that is genuinely contested.** Whether municipal broadband projects have delivered the promised cost and quality improvements is contested. Some projects have been successful; others have failed financially or operationally. How much of observed U.S. broadband price differences relative to other countries reflects regulatory choices versus population density, geography, and other structural factors is contested. The effect of current federal broadband subsidy programs on deployment and pricing is being studied and the findings are preliminary.

**Values content that the platform will not take a position on.** Whether broadband should be treated as a public utility, a private service subject to regulation, or a competitive market with minimal intervention is a values question about the appropriate role of government in providing infrastructure. How much weight to give rural deployment versus urban affordability is a values question when resources are constrained. Whether internet access should be treated as a basic right comparable to electricity and water is a values question. The platform takes no position on these.

### A note on how these frames will be used

The distinction between empirical and values content is something the platform will try to surface inside the deliberation process itself. When participants deliberate on a specific bill, the briefing materials will try to identify what is empirically settled, what is empirically contested, and what is a values question on which reasonable participants may disagree. We will not always get this right. We expect participants to push back on our framings when they believe we have categorized something incorrectly — treated a values question as if it had an empirical answer, or treated a contested empirical claim as if it were settled.

The commitment from Article 3.1 applies here with particular force: **if you find the platform pre-loading answers to values questions in any of the framings above or in any briefing material the platform publishes, that is a design failure. Tell us.** The credibility of the deliberation process depends on participants being able to trust that the framing is honest, and that trust cannot be maintained without external correction.

We will also be explicit about one thing the platform *will* do that could be mistaken for taking a position. When a platform deliberation produces a specific policy position — say, support for a particular amendment to a particular bill — the platform will carry that position forward into the advocacy work, because that is the whole point of the theory of change. This is not the platform taking a values position. It is the platform acting as a vehicle for the positions participants have reached through structured deliberation. The distinction matters: the platform is pro-deliberation and pro-follow-through on what deliberation produces, not pro any particular substantive outcome.

### What this means for deliberation

The examples above are meant to show that the empirical/values distinction is workable in practice but not mechanical. Every policy area has its own structure, and drawing the line between "this is what we know" and "this is what you have to decide" requires judgment calls that can be disputed. The platform's commitment is to make those judgment calls visible — to show the reasoning rather than just the conclusions — and to treat disputes about the framing as first-class contributions rather than as obstacles to the deliberation. If a participant's objection to a framing leads to a better framing, the platform is working. If objections are ignored and the framings calcify, something has gone wrong.
