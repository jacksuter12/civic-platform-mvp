## Article 1.6: Democratic Backsliding — What the Evidence Shows

**Section 1: How Things Work**

The phrase "democratic backsliding" entered the academic literature roughly two decades ago to describe a pattern that did not fit older models of democratic collapse. The classic image of a democracy ending was the **coup**: tanks in the streets, the legislature dissolved, an elected government replaced by a military junta. This still happens in some places, but it has become rarer. What scholars began noticing was a different pattern: democratically elected leaders using legal and constitutional means to gradually concentrate power, weaken checks on the executive, marginalize opposition, and erode the conditions that make future democratic competition meaningful.

The political scientists Steven Levitsky and Daniel Ziblatt, in *How Democracies Die* (2018), describe this pattern as **executive aggrandizement**, a term they borrow from earlier scholarship. The defining features are a slow accumulation of power within the executive at the expense of horizontal accountability institutions — courts, independent agencies, the legislature, the press — combined with the closing of civic space through legal harassment of opposition, restrictions on media, and the cultivation of partisan loyalty among officials whose role is supposed to be neutral. None of these moves looks like a coup. Each one, taken individually, is defensible on some legal or political ground. Taken together, they hollow out the institutional substance of democratic competition while leaving its formal procedures intact.

The largest systematic effort to measure these trends globally is the **V-Dem Institute** at the University of Gothenburg, which publishes annual democracy reports based on expert coding of dozens of indicators across nearly every country. V-Dem distinguishes between several dimensions of democracy — electoral, liberal, participatory, deliberative, egalitarian — and tracks each over time. Their most recent reports document a decade-long global trend in which the number of countries classified as autocratizing has exceeded the number classified as democratizing, with the affected population concentrated in a few large states. **[VERIFY: V-Dem most recent annual figures, exact framing]** The headline figures change each year and should be checked against the current report rather than cited from memory.

V-Dem's methodology has known limitations. **[Contested]** The data depend on expert coders making judgment calls about indicators that do not have purely objective measures. Different coders can reach different conclusions on borderline cases. V-Dem addresses this through aggregation and statistical modeling, but the underlying inputs are not raw observations of the kind that, say, GDP figures attempt to be. This does not invalidate the data; it means the data should be treated as informed expert judgment rather than as direct measurement. Critics point out that this introduces potential biases that purely formal measures would not have. Defenders point out that purely formal measures (Did an election happen? Was the result accepted?) miss exactly the slow erosion of substance that the concept of backsliding is trying to capture.

The Levitsky and Ziblatt framework has itself been criticized. **[Contested]** Their account focuses on elite behavior — what political leaders do, what norms they violate, what guardrails fail — and pays less attention to the structural and economic forces that may be driving the erosion in the first place. Daron Acemoglu and James Robinson, in *The Narrow Corridor* (2019), offer a complementary structural account that emphasizes the long historical process by which states and societies develop the capacity to constrain each other, and the conditions under which that balance can be lost. The two accounts are not exclusive — elite behavior and structural conditions both matter — but they point to different leverage points for anyone trying to resist democratic erosion.

The American case is genuinely contested among political scientists. **[Contested]** Some scholars argue that the United States has experienced clear democratic backsliding by the technical criteria used in the comparative literature: declining trust in elections, the erosion of norms around peaceful transfer of power, the politicization of nominally neutral institutions, the increasing willingness of partisan actors to use legal tools against political opponents. Others argue that what the United States is experiencing is something distinct: extreme polarization, legislative dysfunction, and the displacement of policymaking from the legislature toward the executive and the judiciary, but not backsliding in the sense the term is used about Hungary or Venezuela. These are not the same diagnosis and they imply somewhat different responses. **[We don't know]** which framing will look correct in retrospect.

What is less contested is that the U.S. legislative branch has lost relative capacity over recent decades. Congressional staff levels (committee staff in particular) have not kept pace with the complexity of the issues they oversee. Major policy is increasingly made through executive orders, agency rulemaking, and judicial interpretation rather than through the passage of new legislation. This is not by itself democratic backsliding, but it does mean that the institution most directly accountable to voters — the legislature — has less effective control over policy outcomes than it did a generation ago. The platform's focus on state legislative engagement reflects a judgment that state legislatures, while imperfect, remain a more tractable point of entry than the federal level for the kinds of cross-cutting issues described in the platform's problem statement.

### What this means for deliberation

Whatever language one uses for the current moment, the trend lines in legislative capacity, institutional trust, and elite norm-following do not look healthy. A platform that aims to influence policy through deliberation has to take seriously the possibility that the institutions it is trying to influence are themselves in a slow process of change. This does not make the work less important. It does mean that "produce a good deliberation and the legislature will act on it" is not a complete theory, and that the platform's design has to anticipate the possibility of working in an environment where formal democratic procedures continue while their substance erodes.
