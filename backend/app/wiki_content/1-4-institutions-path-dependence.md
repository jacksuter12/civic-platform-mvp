## Article 1.4: Institutions, Path Dependence, and Why Change Is Slow

**Section 1: How Things Work**

Douglass North, who shared the Nobel Prize in economics in 1993, defined **institutions** as "the rules of the game in a society, or, more formally, the humanly devised constraints that shape human interaction." This definition is broader than the everyday use of the word. An institution, in North's sense, is not a building or an organization. It is any rule — formal or informal — that structures how people interact. The U.S. Constitution is an institution. So is the unwritten norm that senators do not personally insult one another on the floor. So is the practice of tipping in restaurants. All of these are humanly devised constraints that shape behavior in predictable ways.

North's framework distinguishes between **formal rules** (constitutions, statutes, regulations, contracts) and **informal norms** (customs, codes of conduct, conventions, expectations). Both matter, and they often interact in surprising ways. A formal rule that lacks normative backing — a law no one feels obligated to follow — produces selective enforcement and loss of legitimacy. An informal norm without formal codification can govern behavior for centuries until it is suddenly violated and there is no formal mechanism to restore it. The relative weight of formal rules and informal norms varies across societies and across periods within a society.

The most important concept in North's framework, for the purposes of understanding why institutional change is hard, is **path dependence**. The idea is that the choices available at any given moment are constrained by choices made earlier, sometimes much earlier. Once an institution is established and people have organized their lives around it, changing it becomes costly even if a different institution would be more efficient on the merits. The longer an institution persists, the more activity is built around it, and the higher the cost of change.

The classic illustration of path dependence is the **QWERTY keyboard**. The story goes that the QWERTY layout was designed in the 1870s for early mechanical typewriters with specific engineering constraints that no longer apply, that more efficient layouts (like Dvorak) exist, but that QWERTY persists because everyone learned to type on it and switching costs are too high. **[Contested]** This story has itself been challenged. Stan Liebowitz and Stephen Margolis published a sharp critique in 1990 arguing that the alleged efficiency advantages of Dvorak were exaggerated by the original studies, and that QWERTY's persistence may reflect roughly comparable performance rather than dramatic lock-in. The QWERTY example is still useful for illustrating the *concept* of path dependence, but the empirical claim about its inefficiency is not as clean as the story suggests.

A clearer example from American institutions is the **Senate filibuster**. The filibuster is not in the Constitution; it emerged from procedural choices made in the early 1800s, was rarely used through most of the nineteenth century, and gradually accumulated rules and norms that gave it its current form. At various points across the past century, majorities in the Senate have wanted to abolish or substantially weaken the filibuster, and yet it persists in some form. The reasons are several: senators in the minority value it because they may benefit from it later, senators in the majority face uncertainty about whether they will remain in the majority, and the procedural rules for changing Senate rules are themselves shaped by the filibuster. Each successive Senate inherits a structure that constrains its choices, even when the sitting majority would prefer a different structure on the merits.

A more recent example of formal rule change producing unexpected outcomes is the *Citizens United v. FEC* decision (2010), which removed restrictions on independent political expenditures by corporations and unions. The decision did not create the system of well-funded outside spending groups that followed; it created the legal space in which that system rapidly emerged. The new institutional landscape — Super PACs, dark-money 501(c)(4)s, coordinated independent expenditures — was not legislated into existence. It evolved as actors discovered what the new rules permitted and built organizations to exploit them. This is a useful illustration of how a single formal rule change can cascade into a much larger institutional reorganization that no one specifically designed.

How does institutional change actually happen, when path dependence makes the status quo so sticky? The historical institutionalist literature tends to identify a few patterns. **External shocks** — wars, economic crises, technological disruptions — can disrupt existing equilibria and create windows for reorganization. **Coalition shifts** can change which actors hold decisive power and reshape the rules in their favor. **Slow norm erosion** can hollow out informal constraints over decades until a single high-profile violation triggers a cascade. What is rare, in the historical record, is **rational redesign** — sitting down and replacing an existing institution with a better one through deliberate consensus. Most institutional change is partial, contested, and shaped by the prior structure even as it modifies it.

**North's framework is widely used but not without critics.** Some scholars argue that it underweights agency, power, and ideas relative to efficiency-based accounts of why institutions persist. Historical materialists argue that economic structures drive institutional change rather than the other way around. Constructivists argue that ideas and identities shape what counts as a "rule" in the first place. **[Contested]** These debates are active in political science and economic history, and the platform takes no position on them.

### What this means for deliberation

Anyone trying to change an institution should expect resistance disproportionate to the merits of the proposed change. Path dependence means that even bad institutions have stakeholders who have organized their lives around the existing rules, and that those stakeholders will fight to preserve the status quo. This is not corruption. It is the predictable consequence of how institutions accumulate constituencies. A deliberation platform that produces a clear consensus for change is producing useful information, but it is not, by itself, sufficient to overcome path dependence. The harder question is what kind of accumulated pressure can shift an institutional equilibrium, and over what timescale.
